# Challenge 1.05 — The right tool for the job

- **Concept:** AI vs deterministic — judgment work vs rules work
- **Competency:** Delegation
- **Scaffolding:** independent with guided framework
- **Artifact:** the learner's delegation map of their recurring work

## Lesson

1. **Two kinds of machine.** A *deterministic* tool follows rules exactly
   and gives the same answer every time: a spreadsheet formula, a
   calendar reminder, a mail filter, a template. An AI model *judges*: it
   reads, weighs, interprets — brilliantly, but never twice identically.
   Ground each in examples from the learner's own toolkit.
2. **The sorting rule.** If you can fully write down the rules — these
   inputs, these steps, this output, no exceptions worth judgment — a
   deterministic tool is better: faster, free to rerun, auditable, right
   every single time. Use AI where language, judgment, or messy
   unstructured input is involved. Asking AI to do rules-work it could
   systematize is paying judgment prices for arithmetic.
3. **Hybrid is the norm.** Most real workflows split: AI drafts, a
   template formats; a filter sorts, AI summarizes what passed. And the
   best-kept secret — AI is *outstanding at building* deterministic tools.
   That's the entire next challenge; just plant the flag here.

4. **Revisit Challenge 1.** Pull out their guesses about what this
   colleague would be great/worrying at. With the vocabulary they now
   have, re-sort them together — a satisfying, visible measure of how far
   they've come in four challenges.

## Task

1. **Inventory.** The learner lists 8–12 recurring tasks from their
   actual work — the weekly/monthly drumbeat, the annoying ones
   especially. (You may interview them to draw the list out; the tasks
   are theirs.)
2. **Triage.** For each task they assign: **AI** (judgment/language),
   **deterministic** (rules — a tool or template should do this),
   **hybrid** (split it: which part is which), or **human-only** (their
   call: relationships, accountability, things they don't want to
   delegate). With one line of reasoning each.
3. **The artifact.** The result becomes `delegation-map.md` — a table
   they'll act on: each task, its bucket, the reasoning, and for one or
   two: "try this first."

## Parameters

- The inventory is the learner's real recurring work, not hypotheticals.
- The triage reasoning is the learner's; the companion challenges weak
  reasoning ("is that *really* judgment work, or rules you haven't written
  down?") but doesn't assign buckets.
- At least one task lands in each of AI / deterministic / hybrid (if their
  honest inventory can't fill a bucket, they explain why — that's an
  acceptable pass).

## Rubric

1. `delegation-map.md` exists with 8+ real recurring tasks, each bucketed
   with a stated reason.
2. The reasoning shows the sorting rule applied — judgment/language vs.
   fully-specifiable rules — not vibes ("AI because it's hard" fails the
   sniff test; probe it).
3. At least one hybrid task is genuinely decomposed into its AI part and
   its deterministic part.
4. The learner has marked 1–2 "try this first" candidates — and at least
   one deterministic candidate suitable for Challenge 6.

## Companion notes

- This is the curriculum's keystone judgment skill and most challenges
  ahead build on this map — invest in its quality.
- Watch for both failure directions: AI-for-everything (now that the
  hammer is fun) and AI-for-nothing-mechanical (not seeing that a tool
  could absorb their copy-paste ritual). Name whichever you see, kindly.
- The "human-only" bucket deserves respect, not pushback — deciding what
  *not* to delegate is the Delegation competency too.
- End with a hook: pick their most promising deterministic candidate and
  say "next time, you don't do this task — you build the machine that
  does." That's Challenge 6.
- Reflection question at review: "Which bucket surprised you most when
  you sorted honestly?"
