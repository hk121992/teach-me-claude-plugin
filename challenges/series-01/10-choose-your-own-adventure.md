# Challenge 1.10 — Choose your own adventure (Foundations capstone)

- **Concept:** integration — the learner designs and delivers their own project using everything from the series
- **Competency:** all four (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence)
- **Scaffolding:** open
- **Artifact:** a deliverable the learner defined, plus the brief and plan that produced it

## Lesson

Short, because the doing is the point:

1. **They've changed.** Nine challenges ago they'd never briefed an AI
   colleague. Now they own a brief template, a verification checklist, a
   delegation map, a working machine, a memory file with safety rules,
   and the judgment to use them. The capstone proves it — on a goal
   *they* choose.
2. **The professional loop, end to end.** Choose well → brief well →
   decompose → checkpoint → verify → done. This time nobody hands them
   the task.

## Task

The learner defines and completes a project that is genuinely useful to
them. Anything they want, **within these parameters** (state them up
front — they are the rubric's skeleton):

1. **Real.** The result must be something they will actually use:
   a deliverable for their work, a system for a recurring chore, a
   personal project that matters to them.
2. **Briefed.** A written brief before any execution — using their own
   template — including explicit success criteria: how *they* will judge
   it done and good.
3. **Decomposed.** A plan of stages with at least two checkpoints where
   they review before continuing.
4. **Verified.** At least one deliberate verification step, chosen with
   their checklist and matched to the stakes.
5. **Safe.** Their safety rules respected — sensible material, sensible
   scope; the project would survive their own Challenge 9 audit.
6. **Theirs.** The learner directs; you execute and advise. The thinking
   visible in the brief, plan, and verdicts must be recognizably theirs.

Scope guidance: more ambitious than any single earlier challenge,
finishable in one or two sessions. If they dream too big, help them carve
the version-one core; too small, ask what would make it actually matter.

Process: settle the idea together → they write the brief → they sketch
the plan (critique honestly — a weak plan caught now is cheap) → execute
with real checkpoints → review.

## Rubric

Graded against the six parameters above, *plus* the learner's own success
criteria from their brief:

1. Parameters 1–6 each demonstrably met (walk them one by one, with
   evidence from the artifacts and the session).
2. The learner's own success criteria met — or consciously renegotiated
   along the way for stated reasons (that's professional judgment, not
   failure).
3. In the review conversation, the learner can narrate their loop:
   why this task, why this decomposition, what they verified and why
   that check fitted those stakes.

## Companion notes

- This is their moment, not the curriculum's. Coach minimally; let the
  scaffolding be invisible. Intervene only when a parameter is about to
  be violated.
- Grade the capstone honestly — it should feel *earned*. If it's a
  refine, be specific and warm: the gap is usually one parameter, not
  the whole project.
- On pass, run the full series send-off (see the `review` skill): recap
  all ten skills with moments from *their* journey, revisit their
  Challenge 1 `first-impressions.md` together — the distance travelled
  is the best graduation speech — and show them the roadmap for where
  the journey goes next.
- Save their capstone brief to `.learn/progress.json` under
  `capstone_briefs` — later series build on it.
- Reflection question at review: "What will you delegate next Monday
  that you'd have done by hand two months ago?"
