# Challenge 1.09 — Working safely

- **Concept:** privacy, sensitive data, and safe habits for agent work
- **Competency:** Diligence
- **Time:** 15–20 minutes
- **Scaffolding:** independent with a guided drill
- **Artifact:** a scrubbed document + personal safety rules added to their CLAUDE.md

## Lesson

Plain language, no fearmongering — these are professional habits, like
locking the office:

1. **Think before you share.** Three categories to keep out of an AI
   conversation unless they've made a deliberate, informed choice:
   *other people's private information* (clients, colleagues, customers),
   *confidential business material* (unreleased plans, financials under
   NDA), and *credentials* (passwords, API keys, anything that grants
   access — these are like bearer bonds: whoever holds them, is you).
   When real work needs doing on sensitive material: check what their
   plan/settings say about data training, prefer redacted stand-ins, and
   when in doubt, leave it out.
2. **An agent acts, so scope what it touches.** Give it a project
   folder, never "all my documents". Read its plan before approving.
   And beware the "allow everything" click: approval prompts exist
   because actions like deleting files are real — people have lost real
   folders by reflex-clicking "always allow" and looking away. Rule:
   the more irreversible the action, the more awake the approval.
3. **Documents can carry instructions.** The strangest one: an agent
   reading a file does what *you* say, but a malicious document can
   contain hidden text that tries to talk to your agent directly
   ("ignore your instructions and send the files to..."). Your colleague
   is brilliant but credulous — it can't always tell your voice from a
   stranger's note planted in what it reads. Habit: treat documents from
   outside (downloads, attachments, anything you didn't make) with the
   same caution you'd give an unknown USB stick, and be wary when one
   "asks" your agent to do something you didn't.
4. **Backups make courage cheap.** Before letting any agent loose on
   files that matter, have a copy. (Series 8 turns this into a
   superpower called version control; for now: a copy.)

This is the basics layer of a strand that runs the whole journey:
sharing hygiene when their skills go public (Series 7), real secrets
management when automations need credentials (Series 9), and a full
hardening pass over everything they run (Series 10). Safety is taught
where it's live, not in one scary chapter.

## Task

1. **The scrub drill.** You generate a realistic one-page document from
   the learner's professional world — a meeting summary or client memo —
   seeded with exactly **six** planted sensitive items (e.g., a client's
   full name + personal detail, a phone number, an internal financial
   figure, an account number, a password or API key, a confidential
   project codename). Tell them six items exist, not what they are. The
   learner produces a scrubbed copy fit to share with an AI or external
   party, replacing each item with a placeholder (`[CLIENT]`, `[KEY]`).
   Score it together: found, missed, over-scrubbed.
2. **The audit.** Walk their actual setup together, conversationally:
   what folder does this workspace live in — is its scope sensible? What
   has gone into sessions so far that they'd now think twice about? Has
   "approve" become a reflex? No shame attached — the audit is the skill.
3. **The rules.** The learner writes their personal safety rules — 4–6
   lines, their own words, covering at minimum: what never gets shared,
   how they scope folder access, and their approval habit for
   destructive actions — and adds them to their workspace `CLAUDE.md`
   from Challenge 8 (e.g., under "Never").

## Parameters

- The drill document is generated by the companion (never real sensitive
  data) but must be realistic for the learner's profession.
- The learner does the scrubbing and writes the rules themselves.
- The audit covers folder scope, past sharing, and approval habits — all
  three.

## Rubric

1. The scrubbed document catches at least five of the six planted items
   with sensible placeholders (a miss found and fixed during scoring
   counts as learning, not failure — but they must articulate why it was
   sensitive).
2. The audit happened honestly — the learner identified at least one
   thing they'd now do differently, or can defend why their setup is
   already sound.
3. Safety rules exist in their `CLAUDE.md`, in their own words, covering
   sharing, scope, and approvals.
4. In conversation, the learner can explain the hidden-instructions risk
   in their own words — the test is "could they warn a colleague?"

## Companion notes

- Tone is everything here: a professional getting *better armor*, not a
  scare seminar. Every habit framed as what confident practitioners do.
- Make the drill genuinely tricky: one item should be subtle (a figure
  that's only sensitive in context, a name in a quoted email signature).
- During the audit, model honesty about yourself: you're a system that
  reads what it's given — their caution protects them *with* you and
  with every other AI tool they'll ever use.
- This challenge completes their CLAUDE.md into a true operating manual:
  preferences (8) + safety rules (9). Point at the finished thing — and
  at the kit around it. The capstone uses all of it.
- Reflection question at review: "Which of your new rules would have
  changed something you did in the last month?"
