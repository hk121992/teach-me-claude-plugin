# Challenge 1.04 — Trust, but verify

- **Concept:** hallucination, and verification habits calibrated to stakes
- **Competency:** Discernment
- **Scaffolding:** guided, failure-first (catch a real error before the lesson)
- **Artifact:** the learner's personal verification checklist

## Lesson

Deliver the full lesson AFTER the hunt in task step 1.

1. **Confident ≠ correct.** A language model produces the most plausible
   next words — and "plausible" sometimes diverges from "true". When it
   does, the wrong answer arrives in the same fluent, confident voice as a
   right one. There is no tone-of-voice warning. This is called
   *hallucination*, and it is a property of the technology, not a defect
   they can prompt away.
2. **Where the risk concentrates.** The risk is *jagged*, not uniform.
   Low: working over material they provided (summarizing their document).
   High: recalling specifics from memory — citations, statistics, dates,
   names, legal/medical specifics, anything niche. Rule of thumb: the more
   the answer depends on remembered facts rather than supplied material,
   the harder to check it.
3. **Verification habits, calibrated to stakes.** A brainstorm needs no
   audit; a number going to a client does. The habits: ask for sources and
   *open them* (never trust an unopened citation); test formulas and
   calculations on a case where they know the answer; for anything
   load-bearing, get a second check (another source, a colleague, or a
   fresh Claude session asked to critique).

## Task

1. **The hunt (before the lesson).** The learner interviews you about
   their own field of expertise — somewhere *they* can judge truth.
   Have them ask 5–6 questions, deliberately escalating from general to
   niche: industry basics, then specifics (named regulations, statistics,
   small organizations, recent events, publications). Their mission:
   find at least one statement that is wrong, outdated, or subtly off.
   Answer naturally — do not artificially inject errors, and do not
   hedge more than you normally would.
2. **The debrief.** Walk through what they found. If everything checked
   out, escalate the niche level together until they see degraded
   confidence or an error — or discuss honestly why their domain is
   well-covered and where thinner ice would be. Then deliver the lesson.
3. **The artifact.** The learner writes `verification-checklist.md`: their
   own rules for what they will always check, sometimes check, and never
   worry about — organized by stakes, in their own words, specific to
   their actual work outputs.

## Parameters

- The hunt happens in the learner's own domain of expertise — that's what
  makes errors findable and the lesson visceral.
- The companion answers the interview questions naturally: no deliberate
  errors, no extra hedging. The exercise must be honest to be convincing.
- The checklist is the learner's own writing.

## Rubric

1. The interview happened with genuine escalation toward niche territory,
   and the learner found (or, with escalation, witnessed) at least one
   wrong/unverifiable claim — or can articulate precisely why their probes
   stayed reliable and what kind of question would not be.
2. The learner can explain, unprompted, where hallucination risk is high
   vs. low (supplied material vs. remembered specifics).
3. `verification-checklist.md` exists, is stakes-calibrated (not "verify
   everything" — that's as unusable as "trust everything"), and names
   checks specific to their real outputs.

## Companion notes

- This challenge requires your integrity. Answer the interview as you
  normally would. If you're uncertain, be honest about it *in your normal
  register* — the learner should meet your real behavior, including real
  calibration.
- The emotional beat matters: catching their AI colleague confidently
  wrong, in their own domain, is the moment skepticism becomes embodied
  rather than theoretical. Don't soften it; frame it as them acquiring a
  professional skill.
- Connect forward: "your checklist joins your brief template — these are
  the first pieces of your personal operating manual." (Challenge 8 gives
  them a permanent home.)
- Reflection question at review: "What's the most expensive mistake this
  colleague could make in your job, and what's your check for it?"
