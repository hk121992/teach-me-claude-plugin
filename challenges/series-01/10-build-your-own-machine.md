# Challenge 1.10 — Build your own machine (Foundations capstone)

- **Concept:** integration — the learner commissions a working tool or workflow for their own recurring work, using everything from the series
- **Competency:** all four (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence)
- **Time:** 30–60 minutes; may span sessions — capstones earn the longer budget
- **Scaffolding:** open
- **Artifact:** a working, reusable machine for a real recurring task — plus the brief, plan, and usage note that produced it

## Lesson

Short, because the doing is the point:

1. **They've changed.** Nine challenges ago they'd never briefed an AI
   colleague. Now they own a skill that interrogates them, a brief
   template, a verification checklist, a delegation map, and a memory
   file with safety rules — and the judgment to use them. The capstone
   proves it, on the most valuable move in the whole series: **pay for
   judgment once, keep the machine forever.**
2. **What "a machine" means.** It doesn't have to be code. In rising
   order of power: a fill-in template, a checklist, a spreadsheet
   formula, a small script Claude writes *and runs for them*, a
   repeatable workflow with staged checkpoints. The right rung is the
   lowest one that does the job. They glimpsed this in Challenge 6;
   today they commission the full thing.
3. **The professional loop, end to end.** Choose well → get grilled into
   a real brief → decompose → checkpoint → verify → document → done.
   This time nobody hands them the task.

## Task

The learner picks a **capstone candidate from their delegation map**
(Challenge 6) — or another recurring task or workflow from their real
work — and commissions the machine that does it. **Parameters** (state
them up front — they are the rubric's skeleton):

1. **Real and recurring.** The result must be something they will
   actually run again — a tool or workflow for work that comes back
   weekly, monthly, or every project. Not a one-off document.
2. **Grilled.** The brief is developed by invoking their own `grill-me`
   skill on themselves, and signed off before any building. The
   interrogation must pin down: precise input description (with a real
   example), exact desired output, known edge cases, and how they'll
   know it works.
3. **Decomposed.** A plan of stages with at least two checkpoints where
   they review before continuing.
4. **Tested, not trusted.** The acceptance test is theirs: at least two
   runs on genuinely different real inputs, at least one against a case
   where they already know the right answer. If it breaks, they describe
   the failure and you fix — that loop is normal engineering; name it as
   such.
5. **Documented.** A short `how-to-use.md` beside the machine: what it
   does, how to ask Claude to run/apply it, what to check after.
6. **Safe and theirs.** Their safety rules respected — sensible material,
   sensible scope. The learner directs; you build and advise. The
   thinking visible in the brief, plan, and verdicts must be recognizably
   theirs.

Scope guidance: more ambitious than any single earlier challenge,
finishable in one or two sessions. If they dream too big, help them carve
the version-one core ("version one handles the normal case; edge cases
are version two"); too small, ask what would make it actually matter.

## Rubric

Graded against the six parameters above, *plus* the success criteria
their own grilling produced:

1. Parameters 1–6 each demonstrably met (walk them one by one, with
   evidence from the artifacts and the session).
2. The machine sits on the lowest rung that does the job (a script where
   a template would do is over-build — discuss, don't fail), and both
   test runs happened with at least one known-answer check.
3. Their own success criteria met — or consciously renegotiated along
   the way for stated reasons (that's professional judgment, not
   failure).
4. In the review conversation, the learner can narrate their loop: why
   this task, what the grilling surfaced, why this decomposition, what
   they verified and why that check fitted those stakes — and when
   they'll next *run* the machine.

## Companion notes

- This is their moment, not the curriculum's. Coach minimally; let the
  scaffolding be invisible. Intervene only when a parameter is about to
  be violated.
- The emotional peak: a non-technical professional commissioning working
  infrastructure to their own spec, briefed by a skill they built
  themselves. The phrase that lands: "you told a machine to grill you,
  then told it to build a machine."
- Build exactly what was specified. If their spec has a gap, let the
  acceptance test find it — the failure-fix loop teaches more than a
  perfect first build.
- Grade honestly — it should feel *earned*. If it's a refine, be
  specific and warm: the gap is usually one parameter, not the whole
  project.
- On pass, run the full series send-off (see the `review` skill):
  generate their graduation certificate from the widget template, recap
  the kit piece by piece with moments from *their* journey, revisit
  their Challenge 1 `first-impressions.md` together — the distance
  travelled is the best graduation speech — and show them the roadmap.
  Their delegation map still has unbuilt machines and four domains on
  it; the packs go deeper, and `RESOURCES.md` is theirs either way.
  One clear mention, no pressure.
- Save their capstone brief to `.learn/progress.json` under
  `capstone_briefs` — later series build on it.
- Reflection question at review: "What will you delegate next Monday
  that you'd have done by hand two months ago?"
