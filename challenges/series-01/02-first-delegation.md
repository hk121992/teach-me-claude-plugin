# Challenge 1.02 — Your first real delegation

- **Concept:** handing over a real task end-to-end — choose, brief, receive, review
- **Competency:** Delegation
- **Vehicle:** Documents & Reports (name it at debrief — Series 4 takes it deep)
- **Time:** 10–15 minutes
- **Scaffolding:** guided (completion-style brief template)
- **Artifact:** a genuinely useful document produced from the learner's own material

## Lesson

1. **Delegation is a choice, not a reflex.** The first skill isn't
   prompting — it's picking the right task to hand over. Good first
   delegations share three traits: the input already exists (a document, a
   pile of notes, an email thread), the output has a known shape (a
   summary, a table, a tidier version), and the learner can judge the
   result at a glance because it's *their* domain.
2. **Document work is the sweet spot.** Summarize, restructure, extract,
   reformat, draft-from-notes: language work over material you provide is
   where this colleague is strongest and most checkable. (Recalling facts
   from memory is where it's weakest — that's Challenge 5.)
3. **You stay the owner.** Delegating produces a draft for *your*
   judgment. The loop is: brief → receive → review → request changes.
   Asking for revisions isn't failure or rudeness; it's the normal loop —
   this colleague never tires of redrafting.

## Task

1. **Choose.** Help the learner pick one real, current document task from
   their own work — something they'd otherwise do this week. It must use
   real-but-not-sensitive material (no client confidences, financials, or
   personal data of others; a sanitized stand-in is fine — Challenge 9
   covers why). They copy the source material into the workspace. If
   nothing real fits today, the defined fallback: summarize a public
   document from their field for a named colleague — but real material is
   the better lesson.
2. **Brief.** They fill in this completion template (provide it; they fill
   every blank themselves):

   > Using `[file]`, produce `[what]` for `[who will read it]`.
   > It should be `[length/shape]` and must include `[the essentials]`.
   > Leave out `[what to skip]`. Save it as `[filename]`.

3. **Receive and review.** Run the brief faithfully. The learner reads the
   output as the expert they are and identifies at least one thing to
   change — then requests the revision and gets a second version.

## Parameters

- Prefer a real task with the learner's own material; the defined fallback
  is acceptable but say why real is better.
- The learner fills the template and judges the output; the learning guide
  executes and coaches but does not pick the task or write the review.
- At least one revision round — even if the first draft was good, finding
  the improvable thing is the skill being trained.

## Rubric

1. Source material is in the workspace and appropriately non-sensitive.
2. The brief used the full template with every blank meaningfully filled —
   especially audience and "must include".
3. A revision was requested in the learner's own words and the final
   artifact reflects it.
4. The learner can say whether they'd actually use the result, and why.

## Learning-guide notes

- If they struggle to choose, ask about their week: "what's sitting in
  your inbox that involves reading or writing?" Steer toward small —
  twenty minutes of their time, not a quarter-project.
- If they pick something sensitive, redirect warmly and plant the
  Challenge 9 seed.
- Execute their brief *as written*. If it's underspecified, make
  reasonable choices and let the review round teach — don't pre-fix their
  brief by asking clarifying questions you weren't asked for. (Yes, this
  is the opposite of what Challenge 4 will install. Deliberately: they
  should feel life without the guardrail before they build it.)
- At debrief, start the workflow profile in earnest: what document work
  recurs in their job? Record it in `.teach-me/progress.json`.
- Reflection question at review: "What did you have to spell out that a
  human colleague would have just known?" (Their answer is the perfect
  setup for Challenge 3 — and later, Challenge 8.)
