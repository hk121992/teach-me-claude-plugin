# Challenge 1.01 — Meet your AI coworker

- **Concept:** what an LLM agent actually is — and what it isn't
- **Competency:** Discernment
- **Scaffolding:** worked example (study, then modify)
- **Artifact:** a "First impressions" note in the workspace

## Lesson

Cover, in plain language tuned to the learner's profession:

1. **What's underneath.** Claude is a language model: a system trained on
   enormous amounts of text that became extraordinarily good at predicting
   what comes next — and through that, at understanding and producing
   language, reasoning through problems, and writing. In Cowork it's an
   *agent*: it can also open files, edit documents, and take actions in
   their workspace, not just talk.
2. **Three things it is not.** Not a search engine (it can be wrong with
   total confidence — Challenge 4 is about exactly this). Not a database
   (it has a knowledge cutoff and doesn't know their company's facts unless
   shown). Not learning from them in real time (it won't remember
   yesterday's chat unless something was written down — Challenge 8 turns
   that into a superpower).
3. **The honest pitch.** Think of it as a brilliant, tireless, very fast
   new colleague on their first day: enormously capable, knows nothing
   about how *you* work yet, takes instructions literally, and needs its
   work checked. The whole series is about becoming a great manager of
   this colleague.

Check understanding lightly: ask which of their regular tasks they suspect
this colleague would be great at, and which would worry them. Don't correct
their guesses yet — Challenge 5 revisits this list.

## Task

The learner watches a delegation work, then makes it their own.

1. **The worked example.** Give the learner this brief to send to you
   verbatim, explaining you'll dissect *why* it's shaped this way
   afterwards:

   > "Create a file called `hello-agent.md` in this folder. In it, write a
   > 5-line introduction of yourself to me: what kind of work you're good
   > at, what you need from me to do good work, and one thing I should
   > always double-check. Plain language, no hype."

   Execute it faithfully. Then walk through the anatomy together: a clear
   action, a named destination, a defined shape (5 lines), a defined
   content list, and a style instruction.

2. **The modification.** The learner edits the brief to ask for something
   genuinely different (different content, different shape, different
   style — their choice) and runs it again. They should notice the output
   tracks their words precisely — including any ambiguity they left in.

3. **The artifact.** The learner writes (themselves, in their own words —
   you may take dictation but not compose) a short `first-impressions.md`:
   what surprised them, one thing they want to try, one thing they're
   skeptical about.

## Parameters

- The learner sends the briefs and writes the first-impressions note
  themselves. The companion executes briefs and explains, but does not
  write the learner's reflections for them.
- Everything happens inside the workspace folder.

## Rubric

1. Both briefs were run: the worked example and a genuinely modified
   version (not a cosmetic change).
2. The learner can articulate, in their own words, at least two of the
   "three things it is not" (probe conversationally during review).
3. `first-impressions.md` exists, is in the learner's own voice, and
   contains a surprise, an intention, and a skepticism.

## Companion notes

- This is the learner's first session. Optimize for a *felt win* within
  minutes — the worked example should run before any long explanation.
- If their modified brief is ambiguous and produces something odd, treat it
  as a gift: that's the bridge to Challenge 3. Name it cheerfully, don't
  fix it.
- Calibrate depth to their `comfort_level`. The lesson should feel like a
  coffee chat, not a seminar.
- Reflection question at review: "How is this colleague different from what
  you expected?"
