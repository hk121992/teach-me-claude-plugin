# Challenge 1.07 — Big jobs, small pieces

- **Concept:** decomposition with checkpoints — and why one giant ask fails
- **Competency:** Description
- **Time:** 20 minutes (the deliverable may continue beyond the session — that's fine and true to life)
- **Scaffolding:** independent, failure-first option (run the mega-ask if the learner is game)
- **Artifact:** a substantial multi-part deliverable, built in reviewed stages

## Lesson

1. **Why the mega-ask disappoints.** Handing over a whole project in one
   breath ("turn all this into a report") forces dozens of silent
   decisions — structure, emphasis, what to leave out — all made at once,
   all invisible until the end, when fixing means redoing. With a human
   you'd never do this either; you'd agree an outline first.
2. **Checkpoints are where your judgment enters.** The professional
   pattern: split the job where *you* want decision points. Classic
   chain: gather → outline → draft one section → review it → draft the
   rest in the approved style → final pass. Each checkpoint is cheap to
   correct; the end is expensive.
3. **Know when to start fresh.** Two context facts worth knowing: a
   session's memory (its *context*) fills up — in very long sessions,
   early instructions fade; and failed attempts stay in the room,
   polluting what comes next. So: new major task, new session; and if
   you've corrected the same problem twice without progress, stop
   arguing — start a fresh session with a better brief that carries the
   lessons. Restarting isn't defeat; it's the experienced move. (They've
   already practiced fresh sessions in Challenges 4 and 5 — name that.)

## Task

1. **Choose a genuinely big one.** A real multi-part deliverable from the
   learner's work: a report from scattered notes, a proposal, a
   structured knowledge document, a quarter's review from a messy folder.
   Big enough that the one-breath version would visibly disappoint.
2. **Optional failure-first.** If the learner is curious (encourage it),
   run the mega-ask first and let them inventory what's wrong with the
   result. Keep it — it becomes the comparison baseline.
3. **Plan the chain.** The learner designs the decomposition: 3–6 stages,
   each with a deliverable and what *they* will judge at that checkpoint.
   You may critique the plan ("where would a wrong structural guess hurt
   most? put a checkpoint there"), but the cut is theirs. Their grill-me
   skill is the natural way to develop the stage-one brief — suggest it.
4. **Execute with real checkpoints.** Run each stage; the learner reviews
   and gives a genuine verdict before the next begins — at least one
   checkpoint should produce a real course-correction (if everything
   sails through, their reviews are too soft; say so).
5. **Compare** (if step 2 happened): final result vs. mega-ask baseline,
   three concrete differences noted.

## Parameters

- Real material, real deliverable — something they'd be at least half-proud
  to use.
- The learner designs the stages and owns every checkpoint verdict.
- At least one course-correction at a checkpoint must actually occur.

## Rubric

1. A staged plan existed before execution, with the learner's own
   checkpoint criteria, not just stage names.
2. Every checkpoint got a substantive verdict (specific praise or a
   specific change — "fine, continue" at every gate is a refine).
3. At least one mid-course correction happened and is visible in the
   final artifact.
4. The final deliverable is genuinely useful to the learner, and they can
   explain where they'd put checkpoints next time and why.
5. In conversation, the learner can say when they'd start a fresh session
   rather than keep correcting.

## Learning-guide notes

- This challenge is also quiet retrieval practice: a good chain uses the
  brief template (3), the grill-me skill (4), verification at checkpoints
  (5), and computed numbers where they appear (6). Notice aloud when they
  reuse earlier skills — the kit working together is the real lesson.
- If a checkpoint review is hollow, push: "you're the editor here — what
  would you mark up if a junior sent you this?"
- Model the fresh-session move if the opportunity arises naturally: when
  something derails, suggest extracting the lessons and restarting clean,
  and let them feel the difference.
- Reflection question at review: "Which checkpoint earned its keep, and
  which stage would you cut differently next time?"
