# Challenge 1.03 — Say what you mean

- **Concept:** the brief — context, audience, format, success criteria
- **Competency:** Description
- **Scaffolding:** guided, failure-first (run the weak brief before teaching the fix)
- **Artifact:** a side-by-side comparison plus the learner's personal brief template

## Lesson

Deliver this lesson AFTER step 1 of the task — the failure comes first.

1. **Vague in, generic out.** The model can't read minds; given a vague
   request it fills every gap with the most statistically average choice.
   Generic output usually isn't the AI being weak — it's the brief being
   hollow.
2. **The four loads a brief carries.** **Context** (what's the situation,
   what does it need to know), **audience** (who reads this and what do
   they care about), **format** (shape, length, structure, tone),
   **success criteria** (how will you judge it — what must be true of a
   good result). The learner already discovered fragments of this in
   Challenges 1–2; now it gets named.
3. **Talk like an expert briefing a capable newcomer.** Not magic
   keywords — just the things you'd tell a smart freelancer on day one.
   More relevant specifics nearly always beat clever phrasing.

## Task

1. **The failure (run first, before the lesson).** The learner picks
   another real document task and deliberately briefs it the lazy way —
   one vague sentence ("summarize this", "make this better"). Run it
   faithfully. Let them read the result and say what's wrong with it.
   *Then* deliver the lesson.
2. **The diagnosis.** Together, name what was missing in the lazy brief
   using the four loads. Keep their words; map them to the terms.
3. **The rewrite.** The learner rewrites the brief carrying all four
   loads. Run it. They compare outputs side by side and note the
   differences in a short `before-after.md`.
4. **The template.** The learner distills their own reusable brief
   template — their phrasing, their work, 4–8 lines — saved as
   `my-brief-template.md`. This artifact gets used for the rest of the
   series.

## Parameters

- Same source material for both runs — the brief is the only variable.
- The vague brief must actually be run; no skipping to the good version.
- The template is written by the learner in their own words (the four
  loads must be recognizable in it, however they phrase them).

## Rubric

1. Both runs happened on identical material; outputs were genuinely
   compared, with at least two specific differences named in
   `before-after.md`.
2. The improved brief identifiably carries all four loads.
3. `my-brief-template.md` exists, is in the learner's own voice, and would
   make sense to them in a month.
4. In conversation, the learner can name what each of the four loads does.

## Companion notes

- Resist improving the lazy brief — execute it with statistically average
  blandness. The gap between the two outputs IS the lesson; the wider it
  lands, the better it sticks.
- If their "vague" brief is accidentally decent, grin and say so — then
  have them strip it down further. The exercise needs a real baseline.
- When they write the template, push for *their* vocabulary ("who's it
  for" beats "audience" if that's how they talk).
- Reflection question at review: "Which of the four do you naturally
  include, and which one will you forget without the template?"
